1. The "Signal" (Database)
When the SOS is pressed, your app saves a record to the Supabase alerts table. This is the official "trigger" that tells the system an emergency is happening.

2. Track A: The "Radio" (Realtime)
How: Supabase acts like a radio tower. Because your app has a "Listener" turned on, Supabase broadcasts the data to every active device on the global-alerts channel.

Result: If a responder has the app open, they get an instant popup and vibration. This is the fastest way to communicate.

3. Track B: The "Wake-up Call" (Push Notifications)
How: If the responder's phone is in their pocket or the app is closed, the "Radio" won't reach them. Instead, your app tells Expo's servers to send a notification.

Result: Expo sends a signal to Google or Apple, which forces the phone to light up with a lock-screen notification.

Why the New York vs. Taiwan thing happens:
Right now, you are using a Global Frequency. It’s like a walkie-talkie where everyone in the world is on Channel 1.

Taiwan user transmits on Channel 1.

New York user is listening on Channel 1.

Result: The New York user hears the "broadcast" because they are tuned to the same global frequency.

Summary: 1. Supabase holds the data.
2. Realtime handles users who are currently using the app.
3. Push Notifications handle users who have their phones put away.
